1The bigger picture

Good afternoon. I want to start at the level the committee actually cares about — not what I did in the last paper, but what problem I think is worth five years of my PhD to attack. The problem is this. Computational simulation of physical systems — fluids, heat, electromagnetics, solid mechanics, climate, biomedical — is a multi-billion-dollar activity, and it's being rebuilt on learned components. Neural surrogates are replacing classical solvers at every level of the stack: whole-solve surrogates like FNO and DeepONet, learned sub-grid closures for turbulence, learned reduced-order models for design-space exploration. The neural versions are already winning on inference speed. They are losing, badly, on everything else.

The gap is not a gap in accuracy on training data — everyone's surrogate reaches a percent or two. The gap is in what classical numerical analysis has been quietly building for two hundred years: convergence proofs, stability conditions, error bounds, constraint preservation, scaling to adaptive meshes, scaling to thousands of MPI ranks. Those are what make classical solvers trustworthy at deployment. Neural surrogates almost none of these. This is why the FDA will not certify a neural cardiac model. It's why the DOE will not put a learned closure in a climate run that matters. The field is stuck at demos.

My thesis is about fixing that — systematically, chapter by chapter, one guarantee at a time. I think this is one of the most consequential open problems in scientific computing right now, and I want to spend my PhD working on it.

the two gaps the thesis attacks
The trust gap

Training minimizes reconstruction error. Deployment needs Jacobian regularity, spectral stability, constraint preservation, error certification — properties the training loss does not enforce. Today, the learned surrogate works or doesn't work, and we find out the hard way.

The scale gap

Learned ROMs have been demonstrated almost entirely at small, uniform, single-node scale — often a few tens of thousands of degrees of freedom, always a fixed grid. Real scientific computing runs at millions of degrees of freedom on adaptively refined meshes, distributed across thousands of MPI ranks. These two worlds have not met.

2The thesis, in one sentence

Here's the claim the whole PhD rests on, in one sentence. I'll unpack it for the next thirty minutes, but this is what I want you to be able to state back to me when I'm done.

Thesis

Learned reduced-order PDE solvers succeed in deployment only when the learned components' architecture satisfies structural properties required by the online solver — properties distinct from and sometimes orthogonal to training-time reconstruction accuracy — and the systematic identification, architectural guarantee, and computational scaling of those properties is the path from neural surrogates as research artifacts to neural surrogates as engineering tools.

Three claims stacked up in that sentence. First: training objective and deployment requirement are different things. Second: the fix should be architectural — baked into the network structure, not into the loss or the solver — because architectural guarantees hold for all inputs, always. Third: the research program is not one architecture; it is a systematic search across the properties that matter, with one chapter per property class. I'll walk through the five chapters next. One is done. One is in progress. Three are ahead of me.

3Why this is a CS problem, and why now

A question I want to address before the chapters: is this really a computer science thesis, or am I doing applied math? My answer is that this is exactly the kind of problem CS is good at and applied math isn't. Applied math gives you the numerical-analysis theorems — Lax-Milgram, Céa, Babuška-Brezzi, the Strang lemma. Every one of them says “if your discretization satisfies property $X$, then the error is bounded by $Y$.” That's a hundred-year program, and it's beautiful. CS takes that template and asks a different question: what does it take to build a system whose architecture guarantees property $X$ by construction, at the scales modern ML operates at?

Three reasons the time is right. Economic: every simulation-heavy industry — aerospace, biomedical, materials, climate — is trying to adopt ML surrogates and getting burned by surrogates that work in the demo and fail in production. Scientific: neural surrogates have no error-analysis theorems comparable to POD-Galerkin, and the field knows it — you can't certify what you can't bound. Methodological: the ML community has converged on “scale plus data equals competence,” and the intrusive-learned-solver world has spent five years showing that for PDEs this doesn't work. Architectural constraints beat data scaling, for a structured reason. That's a CS insight worth pressing on.

three framings the committee might respond to
Economic. Learned surrogates are failing in production because training rewards one thing and deployment needs another. Closing the gap is the path to certified surrogates.
Scientific. Classical numerical analysis has theorems of the form “discretization property $\Rightarrow$ error bound.” Neural surrogates need the same. Each thesis chapter is one such theorem.
Methodological. Architectural constraints, correctly chosen, beat data scaling on structured problems. This is a CS lesson that generalizes beyond PDE surrogates.

4Chapter 1 — Jacobian regularity (the NeurIPS paper, done)

This is the chapter I have in hand. It is the first concrete instance of the program: identify a property the online solver needs, design an architectural device that guarantees it, validate the device broadly enough to show the principle is not problem-specific. The property is Jacobian regularity of the decoder. The architectural device is a linear skip connection over a CP-tensor decoder. The validation is eight PDE benchmarks across elliptic, parabolic, and advection-dominated regimes, in 2D and 3D, at full-order dimensions up to about seven hundred thousand.

Why this property, why this device, why this breadth — I'll go through quickly. The slower walkthrough lives on the paper page and I'm happy to take any of it in Q&A.

The failure mode that started this chapter

I trained a CP-tensor decoder for 3D Poisson. It reached 1% reconstruction error on held-out data. I ran the online Gauss–Newton solve on four unseen parameter triples, cold-start, from $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{0}$. Two of the four diverged. The difference between the two that converged and the two that didn't was not the training data, not the test distribution, not the optimizer. It was that cases 2 and 3 corresponded to regions of latent space where the decoder Jacobian was ill-conditioned, and Gauss–Newton could not traverse those regions from a cold start. Reconstruction accuracy and Jacobian regularity are independent properties. The field had been optimizing the former and hoping for the latter. I thought this was worth making explicit.

What Gauss–Newton actually uses

The online solver minimizes the PDE residual $\Phi(\mathbf{z}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\|K D(\mathbf{z}) - \mathbf{F}\|^2$ in the latent variable $\mathbf{z}$. One Gauss–Newton step is

$$ (J^\top K^\top K J)\,\Delta\mathbf{z} \;=\; -J^\top K^\top (K D(\mathbf{z}) - \mathbf{F}), \qquad J = \partial D / \partial \mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times k}. $$

The decoder Jacobian $J$ appears twice per step. If $J$ loses column rank anywhere along the iterate path, the reduced Hessian $J^\top K^\top K J$ becomes near-singular and Gauss–Newton diverges. Whether the solver works is a statement about the behavior of $J$ as a function of $\mathbf{z}$ — not about its values at training points.

The architectural fix, stated as a proposition

Proposition 1 (Chapter 1)

Let the decoder use the linear-skip rank code $\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{z}) = W_{\text{dir}}\mathbf{z} + W_{\text{rank}}\,\sigma(W_2\,\sigma(W_1\mathbf{z}))$ followed by a CP-tensor contraction. If $W_{\text{dir}}$ has full column rank after training, then the Gauss–Newton Hessian $J^\top K^\top K J$ has bounded condition number uniformly across $\mathbf{z}\in\mathbb{R}^k$.

The proof is in the paper's appendix B. The intuition is that the linear skip $W_{\text{dir}}\mathbf{z}$ contributes a constant, full-rank term to the rank-code Jacobian $\partial \mathbf{h}/\partial\mathbf{z}$, which the nonlinear correction can perturb but cannot cancel. A deep MLP without the skip has no such guarantee; its Jacobian can and does collapse in test-time regions of latent space.

Anticipated question

Your validation is eight benchmarks, but every benchmark is a structured grid with smooth solutions. What happens when you hit shocks, sharp fronts, unstructured meshes?

Exactly the right question, and it's why Chapter 2 is about AMR with quadtree decompositions. Chapter 1 establishes the principle on structured grids; Chapter 2 shows the principle survives when the grid adapts to the solution. I'll walk through that next.

5Chapter 2 — AMR via quadtrees + parallel solving (in flight)

Chapter 2 is where the thesis stops being a single-machine single-grid exercise and meets real scientific computing. Two connected problems: adaptive mesh refinement, and parallel solving. Both are things every classical PDE solver at scale already does. Both are things no learned-decoder ROM in the literature has done well. This chapter builds the machinery so that Chapters 3, 4, and 5 can assume it.

What AMR means for a learned decoder

Classical AMR is straightforward in principle: refine the grid where the solution has structure, coarsen where it doesn't. Octrees in 3D, quadtrees in 2D. The refinement criterion is a local error estimator; the data structure is a tree; the solver at each level respects the tree topology. This is seventy years of numerical analysis. A learned decoder has none of it. The decoder, as built in Chapter 1, has a fixed output grid. You trained it at $64^3$; it outputs at $64^3$. If the solution has a sharp gradient in one cubic centimeter and smooth behavior everywhere else, you pay for the full $64^3$ output — you cannot spend your budget where it's needed.

The question Chapter 2 asks is: how do you train and deploy a decoder whose output grid is a quadtree, not a uniform lattice? There are at least three distinct architectural directions, and a chapter of research is figuring out which one is right.

three architectural directions for quadtree-AMR decoders
Hierarchical CP. One CP core per tree level, with level-to-level correction terms. Output at a leaf is the sum of coarse-level CP contractions down the path. Natural extension of Chapter 1's CP machinery; clean per-level Jacobian bounds; but expressiveness at fine levels is tied to coarse-level rank.
Coordinate-based decoder. Replace $\hat{u}[i,j,\ell]$ with $\hat{u}(x,y,z)$ — a learned function of a continuous query point, SIREN- or NeRF-style. Evaluates at any leaf cell for free. Loses the CP partial-evaluation structure; need a different path to the Jacobian guarantee.
Multi-resolution latent. One latent code per tree level; solve latent Gauss–Newton hierarchically coarse-to-fine. Matches how multigrid solvers already work classically. Chapter 1's architecture wraps each level.

My current bet is on hierarchical CP. It preserves the $\mathcal{O}(R)$-per-node partial evaluation that makes Empirical Quadrature cheap, and I can state a Jacobian-regularity analog of Proposition 1 for each tree level. The cost structure also composes: quadtree partitioning via Z-order or Morton curves gives you a natural parallel decomposition, which sets up the parallel-solving half of the chapter.

What parallelism looks like in this setting

The online Gauss–Newton solve in Chapter 1 is sequential: evaluate the decoder at the EQ sample nodes, assemble the residual and the Jacobian rows, solve a small $k\times k$ system, update $\mathbf{z}$. The per-iteration cost is independent of $N$, which is what gives you the speedup. But the per-node evaluation, the residual assembly, and the Jacobian-row assembly are all embarrassingly parallel across the sample nodes. Parallelism here is not about making the latent solve bigger; it is about making the data-assembly phase fit on a modern compute substrate — multi-GPU on a single node, eventually MPI across nodes.

I am deliberately not committing to a specific target scale — single-node multi-GPU versus MPI across a cluster — at this stage. The chapter's deliverables are the architectural pieces and the performance analysis; the scale target is set by the PDE benchmarks we pick.

Why this chapter is the load-bearing one in the thesis

Without Chapter 2, Chapter 1's eight-PDE result is a lovely methodological contribution that lives on $128^3$ and never reaches a problem a practitioner cares about. With Chapter 2, the architecture inherits the scaling pathway that classical solvers have earned. That is the difference between a PhD that a theory committee member can love and a PhD that an HPC-systems committee member can also love. I want both.

Anticipated question

You listed three architectural directions for AMR. How long do you give yourself to pick one?

I have a first-cut hierarchical CP implementation running now on 2D Poisson with a quadtree refined at a hard-coded pattern. The chapter's first milestone is: pick a data-driven refinement criterion, demonstrate end-to-end solve on a problem where AMR actually helps (sharp-front Burgers is the canonical candidate), and submit a paper within six months. If hierarchical CP blocks on expressiveness, coordinate-based is the fallback. I would not commit more than nine months before publishing the first AMR result.

6Chapter 3 — Constraint preservation

Chapter 3 generalizes Chapter 1's principle to a different property class. Chapter 1's property was Jacobian regularity — a condition on the derivative of the decoder. Chapter 3's property is algebraic constraint preservation — a condition on the decoder's output values. Incompressibility for Navier–Stokes: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$. Divergence-free magnetic fields in MHD. Mass conservation in reacting flows. Symplectic structure in Hamiltonian systems. Each is a hard algebraic constraint the classical solver respects by construction and the learned solver, trained with reconstruction loss, will violate.

The architectural question is: what decoder structure guarantees the constraint holds exactly in the online solve, not just approximately on training data? For incompressibility specifically, the classical trick is to work with a stream function or a vector potential, which makes the velocity field divergence-free by construction. A learned decoder can do the same: output the potential, take its curl inside the architecture. For other constraints the answer is different — Lagrangian-enforced training, projection layers, or structure-preserving decoder families.

This is one chapter in the shape of Chapter 1: identify the property, find the architectural device, demonstrate it broadly. But the property class is different, so the device is different, and the generalization says something about how the thesis-level principle — architectural guarantee over loss-based enforcement — repeats across property classes.

Anticipated question

Is Chapter 3 just Chapter 1 with a different constraint, or is there genuinely new machinery?

Genuinely new. Chapter 1's device (linear skip) makes a spectral guarantee — bounded condition number. Chapter 3's device must make an algebraic guarantee — a pointwise identity. These are different mathematical objects and the architectural patterns are different: potential-function parametrization, projection layers, structure-preserving coordinates. What transfers is the meta-principle: guarantee the requirement architecturally, not in the loss.

7Chapter 4 — Error certification

Chapter 4 is the payoff chapter. Given an NM-ROM output $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$, can I compute an upper bound on $\|\mathbf{u} - \hat{\mathbf{u}}\|$ using only online data, without running the full-order method? This is what classical POD-Galerkin has: a posteriori error estimators due to Patera, Rozza, Veroy. They let a certified reduced-basis solver tell a user “the answer is correct to within this tolerance.” Neural surrogates currently have nothing comparable. Without a certificate, you cannot put the surrogate in a pipeline where correctness matters.

The reason Chapter 4 depends on Chapters 1 through 3 is that a posteriori bounds for nonlinear-manifold solvers require structural properties of the decoder. You need the Jacobian to behave, you need the constraint residuals to be computable, you need the spatial discretization to be well-defined on the adapted mesh. Chapters 1, 2, 3 supply exactly those. Chapter 4 composes them into a certificate.

If this chapter lands, the thesis has a one-line claim a practitioner can act on: “You can deploy my learned ROM in your pipeline and I can tell you, online, how far its answer is from the true FOM solution.” That's the kind of sentence an FDA regulator, a DOE lab director, or an aerospace certification engineer understands.

Anticipated question

A posteriori bounds for POD-ROM are tight. Will yours be?

Honestly, I don't know yet, and I'm not going to pretend I do. The plan is to start with a Strang-lemma-style bound that uses Chapter 1's Jacobian constants, Chapter 3's constraint residuals, and Chapter 2's mesh adaptation to give a conservative upper bound; then tighten. Classical POD bounds also started conservative and got tighter with ten years of work. If I can deliver a conservative bound with a principled tightening direction, that's already a chapter.

8Chapter 5 — Synthesis on one representative problem

Chapter 5 is deliberately not a deployment chapter with a domain collaborator. I have an ML-side collaboration with a group at Utah, but they are ML people, not a simulation group with a deployment target. Overpromising a deployment I don't have would be the wrong move for a candidacy.

Instead, Chapter 5 picks one technically representative problem that exercises every previous chapter at once. Candidate: a multi-physics benchmark — thermal-mechanical coupling, or a conjugate heat transfer problem — run on an adaptively refined mesh, with an algebraic constraint (incompressibility or mass conservation), and solved to a certified tolerance. No single chapter's methods would suffice. The full machinery would. That is the thesis's claim to engineering relevance: the chapters are not independent; they compose into something a practitioner could, in principle, use.

10Q&A preparation bank

Questions the committee is likely to press on, and what I'd say.

Program-level

This thesis claims five chapters. Five chapters is a lot. How do you know you're not biting off more than one person can do in five years?

Chapter 1 is done and that's the chapter that establishes the machinery. Chapter 2 reuses Chapter 1's architecture and adds a tree data structure plus parallel assembly — both are well-studied problems, I'm not inventing AMR. Chapter 3 is one case study (incompressibility); I'd add more if time allows but one is enough. Chapter 4 I deliberately scope to a conservative bound, not a tight one. Chapter 5 is a synthesis, not new methodology. The thesis is five chapters but it's three papers' worth of new methodology — Chapters 2, 3, 4 — plus an application. That fits in three and a half years after Chapter 1.

Program-level

Why is architectural guarantee the right framing? Why not just train better, with better losses?

Two reasons. First, architectural guarantees hold uniformly over inputs, including out-of-distribution inputs. A loss term enforces a property on training data and hopes it generalizes; in Chapter 1 I show that hope fails — a 1% reconstruction model diverges on test cases. Second, architectural guarantees are composable: I can stack Chapter 1's Jacobian bound with Chapter 3's constraint preservation and get both. Loss-term compositions interact in opaque ways. This is the systems-design argument for architectural over loss-based.

Program-level

What if someone scoops you on Chapter 2?

Possible, and the thesis survives. The framing — architectural guarantees as a unified program — is the thesis's contribution. Any individual chapter could be scooped and the framing would still be worth the degree. That's why a programmatic candidacy is safer than a single-result candidacy. But in practice AMR for learned decoders is not a hot topic — the ML surrogate community is mostly chasing bigger models on uniform grids — and I have a nine-month window before anything plausible appears.

Chapter 1 technical

How does this compare to Kim–Choi 2022 or to the Willcox group's quadratic manifolds?

Kim–Choi get per-node cost $\mathcal{O}(1)$ via a sparse masked MLP. I get $\mathcal{O}(R)$ via a CP-tensor with a principled Jacobian bound via the linear skip. Kim–Choi have no architectural Jacobian guarantee; I do. Quadratic manifolds (Geelen–Willcox, Barnett–Farhat) achieve a bounded Jacobian by restricting the decoder class to affine-plus-quadratic; I achieve the same property without restricting the decoder class, which means I get more expressiveness for the same guarantee. Same motivation (Jacobian regularity), different architectural answer.

Chapter 1 technical

Why not just use a Fourier Neural Operator? Much faster at inference.

FNO is a regression from parameters to solutions; it is not a PDE solver. It has no residual in the online loop, so it cannot enforce a physics constraint at test time, and it generalizes to new parameters only within its training distribution. An NM-ROM solves the equations at each query — the online Gauss–Newton converges when the residual is zero, not when the regression thinks it should. That is what gives NM-ROMs parameter generalization and certifiable error, and it is why neural operators are in a different class of methods for a different use case.

Chapter 1 technical

Your Proposition 1 assumes $W_{\text{dir}}$ has full column rank. Is that guaranteed by training?

No, and I want to be upfront about that.⚠ 3 Empirically, across every trained model I've checked, $W_{\text{dir}}$'s smallest singular value is an order of magnitude above the nonlinear-path contribution, so the bound holds. But the training procedure doesn't enforce it directly. A natural Chapter 2-or-later extension is a regularizer that makes the assumption hold by construction. For the current paper I verify it empirically and flag the assumption explicitly.

Scope / scale

What does “largest $N$” mean in your scale claim? Barnett–Farhat got to 17 million degrees of freedom.

Right, and I am careful about the qualifier.⚠ 2 Barnett–Farhat use a quadratic manifold, not a learned decoder, and ECSW hyper-reduction rather than Empirical Quadrature. The claim I can stand behind is: largest-$N$ fully 3D learned-decoder NM-ROM with EQ-style hyper-reduction and a parametric sweep in the literature corpus I have surveyed. Each qualifier is load-bearing. If I drop any one, someone has gone bigger. The paper states this qualifier explicitly.

Positioning

Why is this a CS thesis and not an applied math thesis?

Applied math gives you the theorem template — if property $X$, then error bounded by $Y$. CS asks how to build systems that guarantee property $X$ at the scales modern ML operates at. My work is almost entirely about architectural devices, data-structure choices (CP tensors, quadtrees), and solver-assembly patterns — standard CS primitives. The theorems are lemmas; the system is the contribution. That's the CS thesis shape.

Risk

What is the biggest technical risk in the program?

Chapter 4. Error certification for nonlinear-manifold ROMs is genuinely open; I don't know in advance whether the bound I sketch will be usefully tight. I've structured the dependencies so Chapter 4 is last, not because it's least important but because it needs Chapters 1–3 to have any chance. If it fails, Chapter 5 synthesis still stands and the thesis defends on 1–3 plus synthesis. That's the fallback.

Forward-looking

Where do you see this line of work in ten years, independent of your career?

Learned PDE solvers will be routine in scientific computing the way automatic differentiation is routine in optimization today — if, and only if, the field figures out how to give them the guarantees classical solvers earned over the last century. I want to be part of the group that does that. I think architectural guarantees are the right primitive, and I think the chapter-per-property-class structure I've laid out here is a template other people can pick up for properties I don't address. If I'm right, the thesis is a useful early entry in a body of work that many people contribute to. If I'm wrong about the framing, the field will go a different direction and my chapters will still stand as individual contributions. Both outcomes are survivable.
